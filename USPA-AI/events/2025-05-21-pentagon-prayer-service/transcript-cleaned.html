Sermon: "Be Still and Know That I Am God"
Pastor Brooks Podiger

I want to take a few minutes now to encourage you from the word of God, specifically from a text that you may already know. My text for today is Psalm 46:10, which says: "Be still and know that I am God."

By a show of hands, who has heard that text before? "Be still and know that I am God." Pretty much all of us, and for good reason—it is an immensely comforting verse. But I wonder, do you know its actual context?

Psalm 46 is not a psalm about nature walks and sipping lattes, which you might surmise if you just hear "be still and know that I'm God" without any context. No, quite apart from it, Psalm 46 is a psalm written by warriors about being in the midst of war.

Here's the context, beginning in verse 8:

"Come, behold the works of the Lord, how he has brought desolation on the earth. He makes wars to cease to the ends of the earth; he will break the bow and shatter the spear; the Lord burns the chariots with fire. Be still and know that I am God. I will be exalted among the nations, I will be exalted in the earth. The Lord of hosts is with us; the God of Jacob is our fortress."

The call to "be still and know that I am God" is a call to quiet attentiveness of soul—not sipping a latte, but when the bullets are flying around your head. The call to be still and know that I am God is to remember, when you feel the gravity and the burden that you carry every day, to know there is a God in heaven and you are not him—and what a relief.

This exhortation is specifically for you in this room, in a way that us civilians don't understand. What about being under the DoD banner makes it especially necessary to be still and know that the Lord is God?

Two Observations and Four Exhortations
Pastor Podiger (continued)

Observation #1: The Temptation to Think You're in Complete Control

Because of the gravity of your job, because of the power of our weapons, because of the weightiness of the responsibility, all the civilian eyes and allies' eyes on you hoping you'll help us—there's a temptation to think that you're actually in control and responsible for final outcomes, especially for those who issue the commands and do the aiming and the shooting.

Now you do have great responsibility, there is no doubt about that. But you are not ultimately in charge of the world—the Lord is. Our Lord Jesus said in Matthew 10: "Not a sparrow will fall to the ground apart from my heavenly Father." And if our Lord is sovereign even over the sparrow's falling, you can be assured that he is sovereign over everything else that falls in this world, including Tomahawk and Minuteman missiles, including strategy meetings and war room debriefings. Jesus has the final say over all of it.

This is why David, the great warrior king of Israel, declared in Psalm 20:7: "Some will trust in chariots, some will trust in horses, but we will trust in the name of the Lord our God."

So distinguished personnel, be still and know that he is God—which means, by necessary inference, you are not. And what a relief! You don't have to bear the burden of the world; that's God's responsibility. He's far better at it than you are. Yours is to do your duty diligently, faithfully, excellently, as unto the Lord, and then to trust him with the final outcomes of it all. He's king, and he's really good at his job.

Observation #2: The Temptation to Think You're All Alone

It is an honor to be part of the military, but as you know far better than I could ever know, parts of your job can feel very isolating as well: the confidential details that you can't even share with your spouse, the coming deployment that you can't yet tell your kids, the unique mental strain the pressure brings, the PTSD that you suffer in silence with.

It is an honor to be in your position, but parts of it are isolating as well—it's part of the essential sacrifice. And it's when this very real feeling comes that you need to be still and know that the Lord is God, that his eye is upon you, that he walks in lockstep with you every step that you take.

Hear the words again of the great warrior king, King David, when he felt this in Psalm 23: "Even though I walk from time to time through the valley of the shadow of death, I won't fear evil. Why? Because you're with me; your rod and your staff, they comfort me."

Notice King David actively dwelt on, meditated on, sung about these realities—and that's what I want you to do also. And remember, if you are a Christian, if you have trusted in the Lord Jesus Christ, God is not just God to you anymore. How did our Lord teach us to pray? "Our Father." God has become your Father, who loves you with all the love that he has for his son, who knows you in your fears and your joys and your sins and your temptations and your marital conflict far better than you even understand it, and who has promised to never leave you nor forsake you, to be with you even until the end of the age.

God is an attentive, merciful, kind, gracious Father who will very soon receive you into his arms on the final day. So be still and know that that God is God when you feel the temptation to believe you're in complete control or when you feel the temptation to believe that you are all alone. You are not. The Lord is with you; he will comfort you.

Four Practical Exhortations
Pastor Podiger (Four Ways to "Be Still")

1. Bookend Your Day with Scripture and Prayer

In scripture you say "I wish I had a word from the Lord." He gave us a really big book—it's called the Bible. So let God speak to you first word of the day, last word in the evening, and then pray—speak back to him. Bookend your day with scripture and prayer.

2. Have Someone in Your Spiritual Foxhole

You don't just need someone to have your back when you are on the warfield; you need someone to have your back when you're on the spiritual warfield, which is all the time. And I'm not talking about platitudes—"Hey brother, praying for you, hope things are cool." I'm talking about somebody who's mature and who knows you, who you could actually confess your sin to and it would be a safe place and say "I really need specific help. Me and my wife are really struggling. I lashed out at my kids. I need to repent and I need your help." Have someone in your spiritual foxhole. Seek them out, look them in the eyes, use words and say "Please, I need you to pray for me and with me." God gives grace to the humble.

3. Prioritize Weekly Worship

There's nothing more important for the spiritual health of the Christian than the gathering with the saints on the Lord's day. This is where Christ meets most powerfully with his people to strengthen and encourage us in the gospel, to shield us from the flaming arrows of the evil one—which is why Satan doesn't want you to go. But you will go. You will show up, you will prioritize it whenever physically possible, you will bring your children, and you will worship the living God, and you will be still and know that he is God.

4. Make This Monthly Gathering Immovable

The fact that this service is happening today is proof that God's blessing is still on our land, despite our best efforts. So show up, sing loud, pray fervently, and all by faith heed the wisdom of the great founder Samuel Adams when he gave this charge: "May every citizen in the army and in the country have a proper sense of the deity upon his mind and an impression of that declaration recorded in the Bible: 'Him that honoreth me I will honor.'"

Be still and know that I am God. Our Lord and our God, we pray by your Spirit you would impress these things upon us. And now we would pray the way our Lord taught us to pray, saying:

"Our Father, who art in heaven, hallowed be thy name. Thy kingdom come, thy will be done on earth as it is in heaven. Give us this day our daily bread, and forgive us our trespasses as we forgive those who have trespassed against us. Lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil. For thine is the kingdom, the power, and the glory forever and ever. Amen."
